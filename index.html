Background & Setting

The Civil Rights Act was in the background of high discrimination against African Americans. In the 1950s the Civil Rights movement started to gain traction. The movement was to stop segregation and discrimination.

Build-Up

Multiple important events that led up to the Civil Rights Act. This is included protests such as the Montgomery bus boycott and March on Washington, supreme court rulings such as Brown v Board of Education. The act was introduced during John F. Kennedy's presidency; however Lyndon B. Johnson signed it after Kennedy was assasinated.

Thesis

The Civil Rights Act of 1964 was passed to promote multiple forms of equality for African Americans; such as by desegregating public places and removing employment discrimination. The act was created in the background of the Civil rights movement. Segregation and racism was still common especially in the South. The act was Successful in achieving its aims in the end.

Heart of Story

The Civil Rights Act was introduced to the House by Emanuel Celler. John F. Kennedy supported it. After John F. Kennedy was assasinated, Lyndon B. Johnson advocated and pushed it forward. When it went to the senate, there was a filibuster. Everett Dirksen helped break the filibuster eventually. The House then approved the senate amendments. Lyndon B. Johnson signed the bill into Law.

Short-Term Impact

The act desegregated public facilities and schools. The Equal Employment Oppportunity Commission was created to regulated employment and workplace. Voting rights were improved which increased voter participation, allowing minorities such as African Americans to become more active in politics. It also led to opposition, especially in the south, which led to the act slowly being enforced.

Long-Term Impact

The civil rights act led to other legislation such as the Voting Rights Act and the Fair Housing Act. It also increased economic mobility for African Americans which led to lower economic inequality. It also made the labor market more efficient by increasing the employment of minorities. This led to individuals that were more qualified, that previously could not enter the workforce, to contibute to the economy.
