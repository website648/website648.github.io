Background & Setting

The Civil Rights Act was in the background of high discrimination against African Americans. In the 1950's the Civil Rights movement started to gain traction, with the aim of ending segregation and discrimination.

Build-Up

The Civil Rights Act of 1964 involved multiple key events that led up to it. This is included protests such as the Montgomery bus boycott and March on Washington, supreme court rulings such as Brown v Board of Education. The act was introduced during John F. Kennedy's presidency; however Lyndon B. Johnson signed it after Kennedy was assasinated.

Thesis

The Civil Rights Act of 1964 is a law that was passed to promote multiple forms of equality for African Americans; such as by desegregating public places and removing employment discrimination. The act was created in the background of the Civil rights movement. During this time segregation and racism was still common especially in the South. The act was Successful in achieving its aims in the end.

Heart of Story

The heart of the story of the Civil Rights Act is the relentless struggle of activists and ordinary citizens who fought for change. It is the story of courage and perseverance, even in the face of violence, resistance, and adversity. The passage of the Act marked a significant victory for civil rights, as it outlawed discrimination in public places, provided equal access to education, and ensured fair employment practices. It also gave the federal government the power to enforce desegregation policies, setting the stage for further legal battles and social change.

Short-Term Impact

The short-term impact of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 was schools and public areas were desegregated, and. The immediate effects were seen in the desegregation of public facilities, schools, and other institutions. Many African Americans were able to find better employment opportunities, and discrimination in the workplace began to decrease. The Act also led to the creation of the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC), which played a key role in addressing workplace discrimination. However, challenges remained, and the Act's enforcement was slow in some areas.

Long-Term Impact

In the long term, the Civil Rights Act of 1964 laid the foundation for future civil rights advances. It was a crucial step in the fight for racial equality and inspired other legislation, such as the Voting Rights Act of 1965 and the Fair Housing Act of 1968. The Act helped to change public attitudes about race and inequality in the United States, though racial disparities persist to this day. Over time, the Act has continued to serve as a powerful tool in the ongoing struggle for social justice and civil rights for all Americans.
